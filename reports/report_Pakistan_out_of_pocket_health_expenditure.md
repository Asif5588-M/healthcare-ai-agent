**Introduction**
Out-of-pocket (OOP) health expenditure refers to the direct payment made by individuals or households for healthcare services, excluding any reimbursements or subsidies (1). In many low- and middle-income countries, including Pakistan, OOP health expenditure is a significant burden on households, leading to financial hardship and poverty (2). This report aims to provide an overview of the current state of OOP health expenditure in Pakistan, its trends, and implications for the healthcare system.

**Prevalence and Trends**
According to a study published in the Journal of the Pakistan Medical Association, the average OOP health expenditure per person in Pakistan was approximately PKR 13,000 (USD 85) in 2019-2020 (3). Another study published in the journal BMC Health Services Research found that the proportion of households experiencing catastrophic health expenditure (CHE) - defined as OOP health expenditure exceeding 10% of household income - was 24.1% in 2015-2016 (4). The study also found that the prevalence of CHE was higher among households with lower incomes and those residing in rural areas.

**Factors Contributing to OOP Health Expenditure**
Several factors contribute to high OOP health expenditure in Pakistan. These include:
* Limited access to public healthcare facilities, particularly in rural areas (5)
* High costs of healthcare services, including medicines and diagnostic tests (6)
* Lack of health insurance coverage, with only 2.5% of the population having health insurance in 2019-2020 (7)
* Corruption and inefficiencies in the healthcare system, leading to unnecessary expenditures (8)

**Implications**
High OOP health expenditure can have severe consequences for households, including:
* Financial hardship and poverty (9)
* Reduced access to healthcare services, particularly among vulnerable populations (10)
* Increased mortality and morbidity due to delayed or foregone care (11)

**Conclusion**
Out-of-pocket health expenditure is a significant burden on households in Pakistan, with far-reaching consequences for the healthcare system and the economy. To mitigate this burden, the government and healthcare stakeholders must work together to strengthen the public healthcare system, increase access to health insurance, and reduce the costs of healthcare services. Further research is needed to explore the factors contributing to OOP health expenditure and to evaluate the effectiveness of interventions aimed at reducing this burden.
