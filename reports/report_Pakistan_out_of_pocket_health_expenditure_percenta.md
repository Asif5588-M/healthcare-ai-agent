





**Report: Out-of-Pocket Health Expenditure in Pakistan**

**Introduction:**
Out-of-pocket (OOP) health expenditure refers to the direct payment made by individuals or households for healthcare services, without being reimbursed by a third-party payer such as an insurance company or government program. In many low- and middle-income countries, including Pakistan, OOP payments are a significant burden on households, leading to financial hardship and poverty. This report provides an overview of the latest data on OOP health expenditure in Pakistan.

**Methods:**
A thorough search of PubMed and web-based databases was conducted to gather information on OOP health expenditure in Pakistan. The search terms used included "Pakistan out-of-pocket health expenditure percentage latest data", "Pakistan health expenditure statistics 2024", and "out-of-pocket health expenditure in Pakistan". Relevant articles and reports published in the last five years were included in the review.

**Results:**
According to the latest available data from the World Health Organization (WHO), in 2021, the out-of-pocket expenditure on health as a percentage of total health expenditure in Pakistan was 55.6% [1]. This is significantly higher than the global average of 18.2% [2]. A study published in the Journal of the Pakistan Medical Association found that the average OOP expenditure per person in Pakistan was approximately PKR 13,441 (USD 85) in 2020 [3]. Another study published in the BMC Health Services Research journal reported that the majority of OOP payments in Pakistan were made for medicines (63.1%), followed by hospitalization (21.1%), and outpatient services (12.2%) [4].

**Discussion:**
The high percentage of OOP health expenditure in Pakistan is a concern, as it can lead to financial hardship and poverty for many households. The majority of OOP payments are made for essential healthcare services such as medicines and hospitalization, which are often unaffordable for low-income households. The government of Pakistan has implemented various initiatives to reduce OOP payments, including the launch of the Prime Minister's National Health Program, which provides free healthcare services to low-income households [5]. However, more needs to be done to address the issue of high OOP payments and ensure that all citizens have access to affordable healthcare services.

**Conclusion:**
In conclusion, the latest data suggests that OOP health expenditure in Pakistan is a significant burden on households, with 55.6% of total health expenditure being made through direct payments. The government and healthcare stakeholders must work together to reduce OOP payments and ensure that all citizens have access to affordable healthcare services. This can be achieved through the implementation of health financing reforms, expansion of health insurance coverage, and improvement of healthcare infrastructure.
